Project Overview

Nordstrom, one of the largest US retail chains with 620+ stores nationwide, needed to migrate its retail store network from Cisco to Juniper infrastructure. This wasn't just a technology swap — it involved coordinating vendor sequencing, managing complex dependencies, and navigating inherent tensions between network architecture teams and field engineers.

As Senior Project Coordinator, I owned the day-to-day execution, vendor coordination, and most critically, stakeholder relationship management when tensions escalated to a breaking point.

The Challenge

  • Vendor Conflict: The Juniper vendor team and Nordstrom's internal engineering team had fundamentally different approaches to migration — causing friction, blame, and a near-complete project stoppage
  • Scale & Complexity: 620 retail locations with varying network maturity, legacy systems, and unique operational requirements
  • Zero Tolerance for Retail Disruption: Any network downtime during store operating hours impacts sales. Migrations had to happen during tight maintenance windows
  • Trust Breakdown: By mid-project, the vendor and engineering team weren't communicating effectively — each blamed the other for delays
  • Scope Creep Risk: Misaligned expectations nearly derailed the entire program

My Approach & Solution

Root Cause Analysis: I stepped in to understand both sides — not to judge, but to diagnose the real issue. The vendor was approaching migrations like enterprise IT environments; the retail team needed faster, leaner operations. Different frameworks, different language, mutual frustration.

Mediation & Alignment: I facilitated joint working sessions where both teams defined:

  • Shared success metrics (on-time, quality, zero retail disruption)
  • Clear communication protocols and escalation paths
  • Risk management processes that addressed both teams' concerns
  • Regular syncs to surface issues early, not after they festered

Trust Rebuilding: Established a "no blame, solutions-focused" culture. When issues arose, we addressed the problem, not the person. Quick wins built confidence that the partnership could work.

Executive Alignment: Communicated the restart plan to senior leaders with clear accountability and a realistic timeline. Got buy-in that the project was back on track and actually improving.

Execution Leadership: Once trust was restored, I coordinated the remaining 400+ store migrations with tight vendor/engineering collaboration. No major incidents.

Key Outcomes:

  • Project successfully restarted and completed all 620 store migrations on time
  • Zero major incidents during store operating hours — retail operations ran seamlessly
  • Vendor-engineering relationship transformed from adversarial to collaborative
  • Customer (Nordstrom) was so confident in the restart that they expanded project scope with additional infrastructure upgrades
  • Received company award for conflict resolution and leadership under pressure
  • Established a vendor-client collaboration model that became best practice for future programs

Key Learnings

  • Conflict is Often Structural, Not Personal: The vendor and engineering team weren't bad people — they had different operating models. Once I reframed it as "aligned vs. misaligned frameworks" instead of "vendor vs. internal team," we could solve it.
  • Mediation Requires Neutrality: I didn't take sides. I listened, validated concerns from both teams, and focused on shared outcomes. That credibility enabled both teams to trust my recommendations.
  • Communication Infrastructure Matters: Once we had clear syncs, escalation paths, and shared metrics, friction decreased dramatically. Structure prevents misunderstanding.
  • Celebrate Small Wins: After restart, we highlighted every successful migration to rebuild morale and reinforce that collaboration works.
  • Executive Transparency is Critical: Keeping leadership informed that the issue was being solved (not hidden) maintained their confidence in the restart plan.
