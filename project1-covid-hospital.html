Project Overview

In March 2020, as COVID-19 cases surged, the US Army Corps of Engineers launched an emergency program to build field hospitals across the country. These temporary hospitals needed critical network infrastructure to operate — connectivity for patient records, telemedicine, medical equipment, and staff communications. The challenge: stand up network infrastructure across 10 hospital locations in under two weeks, with zero margin for error.

This was a crisis-mode program where execution speed, precision coordination, and 24/7 momentum were non-negotiable.

The Challenge

  • Fast-track Execution: 10 locations needed operational connectivity in 2 weeks — typical rollouts take months
  • Fast-track Execution: 10 locations needed operational connectivity in 2 weeks — typical rollouts take months
  • Weather Crisis Management: Severe winter weather and snowstorms caused significant damage to temporary hospital tents in New York. Led rapid contingency planning to protect equipment, and recover timeline delays without sacrificing quality.
  • Multi-Vendor Coordination: Network vendors, construction teams, medical equipment suppliers, and Army Corps all needed synchronized execution

My Approach & Solution

Strategic Planning & Stakeholder Alignment: I conducted an immediate internal kickoff to align leadership expectations and establish a unified PM approach. Within hours, I convened a comprehensive stakeholder meeting with Army Corps leadership, network vendors, communication equipment suppliers, and hospital administrators to clarify requirements, identify roadblocks, and establish decision-making protocols. In parallel, I negotiated expedited equipment delivery with manufacturers, coordinating emergency air freight to pre-position critical network infrastructure at deployment sites.

Crisis Coordination Protocol: I established a daily 10-minute synchronized meeting with stakeholders, vendors, and field teams to identify blockers in real-time and make decisions immediately — no committees, no delays.

Leadership Updates: Navigated dual reporting cadence: weekly executive briefings to internal leadership on budget, timeline, and program health, paired with stakeholder briefings to hospital and Army Corps partners. Ensured consistent messaging while tailoring updates for different audience priorities.

Vendor Orchestration: Managed vendor dependencies and sequencing — network infrastructure had to be ready before medical equipment arrived. Built a real-time status dashboard to track deployments across all 10 sites.

Key Learnings

  • Crisis Requires Speed Over Perfection: In emergencies, good decisions made fast beat perfect decisions made too late. We optimized for speed while maintaining quality thresholds.
  • People Matter Most: The teams that stayed engaged and felt supported delivered the best results. Regular communication and celebrating small wins made a difference.
  • Pre-Planning Enables Crisis Execution: Having contingencies and pre-positioned resources meant we could move fast when it mattered.
  • Mentoring Under Pressure Develops Leaders: Junior team members stepped up when given expanded responsibility. Crisis was an accelerator for their PM development.
